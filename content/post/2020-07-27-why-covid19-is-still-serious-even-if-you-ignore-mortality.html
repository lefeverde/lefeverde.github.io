Introduction

There has been quite a lot of focus on using the infection fatality rate (IFR)1 of COVID-19 to quantify the disease severity. The first thing to note here is that despite the math being simple, this is surprisingly hard to calculate2. One reason is that the total number of cases is unknown. However even if that were known, the actual number is not static3. With that said, the CDC’s current best guess of the overall IFR is 0.65%. When you further break this down according to age, the estimated IFR of younger people is much smaller.

Considering this, you could rightfully say that risk of death is quite small4, especially in low-risk groups. However, just because the mortality rate may low, it doesn’t mean the disease isn’t serious. Just because you recover from COVID-19, it doesn’t mean you’re unscathed. That’s my biggest fear: it’s not the risk of death that worries me, it’s the risk of surviving but being unable to do the things I love to do. This is explored in the following sections, and why COVID-19 can be debilitating in those who recover.

The effects from COVID-19 can be permanent and severe

Short-term consequences of infection can have long-term impacts

Although 99.35% recover from COVID-19, it doesn’t mean they have or ever will return baseline5. For COVID-19, the recovered group would include people who have had acute kidney and cardiac injuries as a result of infection, but ultimately went on to survive. There are also people who have had strokes. Some have even needed to have limbs amputated due to complications of COVID-19. One young woman needed a lung transplant. These people would also be included in the recovered group.

The concensus is that pretty much all organs are fair game to COVID-19. One of the ways COVID-19 can cause so much havoc is through hypercoagulation6. This would explain why COVID-19, a respiratory disease, could bring on things like strokes7.

Early suggestions of chronic, long term consequences of infections

There’s also the possibility COVID-19 can cause or certainly contribute to chronic conditions. For example, COVID-19 can damage the heart but not to the point where it causes a heart attack. The same thing could happen with the kidneys. This could potentially lead to an increased susceptibility for heart and kidney disease in the future.

I should note here that it isn’t clear just how much of the organ damage caused by COVID-19 is permanent. That is why I used the term suggestions in the sub-section title. The only way to know for sure8 is to wait and see9. That, however, is an answer that literally no-one is satisfied with. My best guess10 is that COVID-19 infection will be a risk factor for some chronic diseases. How much of one? Again, I can only guess here, but I’d imagine the magnitude will have a huge range11.

The effects from COVID-19 can also be mild

Some people never realize they have been infected

I was extremely skeptical of reports back in February and March of asymptomatic infections. At the time, I thought that the majority of reports were incorrect12, any true cases of asymptomatic infections were extremely rare. I was completely wrong about this. It turns out that the same virus that can be severe enough to kill can also be so mild that people don’t even know they have it13.

Right now, the CDC’s best guess is that 40% of infections are asymptomatic. That is, 40%14 of people infected with COVID-19 will never realize they have been infected.

Asymptomatic is not the same as unaffected

In the previous section I described asymptomatic as people who do not realize they have been infected. This doesn’t mean the infection is harmless. In some asymptomatic people, SARS-CoV-2 can apparently cause an active lung infection with the same clinical features as symptomatic infection. It’s likely that much of the damage caused15 is reversible. However, this could lead to permanent lung damage in some people.

Conclusion

It should hopefully be clear that there are many outcomes from COVID-19 that aren’t death, but still really, really bad. At this point in the pandemic, we’ve got a pretty good idea of how this disease manifests in the short-term. The good news is that this knowledge has resulted in improved treatments leading to higher survival rates. The bad news is that COVID-19 can still lead to an extremely reduced quality of life. It’s likely that COVID-19 will contribute to the burden of chronic disease in the future. Unfortunately, it isn’t clear how just how prevelant the serious non-fatal adverse events are for COVID-19. I’ll estimate this in future posts.
