The long hauler phenomena: Why COVID19 mortality is only part of the story

2020-07-27

Introduction

There has been quite a lot of focus on using the infection fatality rate (IFR)1 of COVID19 to quantify the disease severity. The first thing to note here is that despite the math being simple, this is surprisingly hard to calculate2. One reason is that the total number of cases is unkown. However even if that were known, the actual number is not static3. With that said, the CDC’s current best guess of the overall infection fatality rate (IFR) is 0.65%. When you further break this down according to age, the estimated IFR of younger people is much smaller.

Lets forgot about the demographics and stick with a single IFR 0.65% for the moment. Since only 0.65% of people die, then that must mean 99.35% of people will ultimately recover. You can see4 in @ref(fig:naive_binary_mortality) that the recovered dwarfs deaths, to the point where it’s hard to even see the deaths at all. The issue here is that recovery can be misleading. That immediately brings to mind a return to baseline. That’s unfortunately not always the case.
