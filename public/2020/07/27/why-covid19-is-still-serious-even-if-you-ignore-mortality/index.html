Why COVID-19 is still serious even if you ignore mortality

2020-07-27

Introduction

There has been quite a lot of focus on using the infection fatality rate (IFR)1 of COVID-19 to quantify the disease severity. The first thing to note here is that despite the math being simple, this is surprisingly hard to calculate2. One reason is that the total number of cases is unknown. However even if that were known, the actual number is not static3. With that said, the CDC’s current best guess of the overall infection fatality rate (IFR) is 0.65%. When you further break this down according to age, the estimated IFR of younger people is much smaller.

Considering this, you could rightfully say that risk of death is quite small4, especially in low-risk groups. Let’s ignore the mortality rate for the time being and focus only on those who survive: just because you recover from COVID-19, it doesn’t mean you’re unscathed. This is explored in the following sections, and why it can be extremely debilitating in those who recover.

The effects from COVID-19 can be permanent and severe

Short-term consequences of infection can have long-term effects

The recovered group would include people who have had acute kidney and cardiac injuries as a results of COVID-19, but ultimately went on to survive. There are also people who have had strokes. Some have even needed to have limbs amputated due to complications of COVID-19. One young woman needed a lung transplant. These people would also be included in the recovered group.

The concensus is that pretty much all organs are fair game to COVID-19. One of the ways COVID-19 can cause so much havoc is through hypercoagulability5. This would explain why COVID-19, a respiratory disease, could bring on things like heart attacks6.

Early suggestions of chronic, long term consequences of infections

There’s also the possibility COVID-19 can cause or certainly contribute to chronic conditions. For example, COVID-19 can damage the heart but not to the point where it causes a heart attack. For example, say COVID-19 causes some damage to the heart but not enough to actually cause a heart attack. The same thing could happen with the kidneys. This could potentially lead to an increased susceptibility for heart and kidney disease in the future.

I should note here that it isn’t clear just how much of the organ damage caused by COVID-19 is permanent. That is why I used the term suggestions in the sub-section title. The only way to know for sure7 is to wait and see8. That, however, is an answer that literally no-one is satisfied with. My best guess9 is that there will COVID-19 infection will be a risk factor for some chronic diseases. How much of one? Again, I can only guess here, but I’d imagine the magnitude will have a huge range10.

The effects from COVID-19 can be so mild as to go unnoticed

Some people never realize they have been infected

I was extremely skeptical of reports back in February and March of asymptomatic infections. At the time, I thought that the majority of reports of asymptomatic infections were incorrect11, any true cases asymptomatic infections were extremely rare. It turns out I was completely wrong about this. Right now, the CDC’s best guess is that 40% of infections are asymptomatic. That is, 40%12 of people infected with COVID-19 will never realize they have been infected.
